“We don't make Bordeaux. We make Galilee.”

This bottle does not try to be something else. It tries to be a faithful translation of one hillside in Upper Galilee — its soil, its light, its night-cool — into the language of wine.

Cabernet behaves differently here than in the Médoc. Hotter days, sharper nights, limestone closer to the root. We do not correct it toward someone else's benchmark — we listen to it. The blend shifts harvest by harvest, because the hillside never repeats.

The family has worked these four hectares since 2003. Every choice — hand-picking before dawn, ageing in its own cellar at 14 °C — is a refusal of the label “like Bordeaux” in favour of one thing: a wine that could come from nowhere but here.
